Frequently Asked Questions

Mars rover missions raise big questions about exploration, science, technology, and the future of space travel.

Rover and Mars Questions

Why does NASA send rovers to Mars?

NASA sends rovers to Mars because they can move across the surface, study rocks and soil up close, take images, and use scientific instruments to investigate the planet's history. Rovers help scientists learn whether Mars once had conditions that could support life.

What makes a rover different from a lander?

A lander stays in one location after touchdown. A rover can drive to different areas, allowing scientists to compare rocks, soil, slopes, craters, and other features across a wider region.

Did Mars ever have water?

Evidence from orbiters, landers, and rovers shows that water shaped parts of Mars in the past. Rovers have studied minerals, layered rocks, and ancient environments that suggest liquid water once existed on the surface.

What did Spirit and Opportunity discover?

Spirit and Opportunity found important geologic evidence related to Mars' watery past. Opportunity, in particular, studied rocks and minerals that helped scientists understand ancient water activity on Mars.

What is Curiosity studying?

Curiosity studies the geology and climate of Mars. Its mission focuses on whether Gale Crater once had environmental conditions that could have supported microbial life.

What is Perseverance doing on Mars?

Perseverance is exploring Jezero Crater, searching for signs of ancient life, studying rocks, and collecting samples that may be returned to Earth by a future mission.

Can humans live on Mars right now?

Humans cannot safely live on Mars without advanced technology. Mars has a thin atmosphere, extreme temperatures, radiation risks, and no breathable air. Robotic missions help NASA study how future human exploration might be possible.

How do rovers communicate with Earth?

Mars rovers send data through antennas. Some communication goes directly to Earth, but much of it is relayed through orbiters around Mars, which then transmit the data back to mission teams on Earth.

Why It Matters

Mars rover missions help answer one of humanity's biggest questions: Are we alone? They also test technology, inspire students, and expand our understanding of planets beyond Earth.
